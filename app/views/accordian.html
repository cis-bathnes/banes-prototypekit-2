Ecology and protected species

If your proposed development is in an area with protected or priority species, or of Special Scientific Interest, you may have to get an expert report on the likely impact on local wildlife. For householders or small-scale developments, this map-based wildlife checking tool will tell you if you should consult an expert before submitting a planning application. 

For major developments, a formal Environment Impact Assessment is always necessary. You can find out how this process works on the Gov.UK website. 

You can learn more about ecological assessment by visiting the Biodiversity in Planning website or viewing their video on the Planning Portal. For notes about local areas, see our page on Biodiversity and geological conservation. 

Trees

As a planning authority, we recognise the environmental and social value of trees. We are directly responsible for maintaining thousands of trees in the public parks and other land we take care of. We have statutory powers to protect the following groups of trees:

  • All trees within Conservation Areas
  • Important trees and wooded areas throughout our district

Tree Preservation Orders (TPOs)

We can protect everything from areas of woodland to single trees or hedgerow trees (but not hedges, bushes or shrubs) with a TPO. These are designed to protect mature trees which are an important part of the character of a place. Once the TPO is in force, it becomes an offence to cut down, top, lop, uproot, wilfully damage or destroy the tree without our permission. If you would like more detail on the full legislation around tree protection, you can find it on the GovUK website.

Trees in a Conservation Area

If you live in a Conservation Area and you want to reduce, fell or do similar works to trees, you will need to notify us six weeks in advance of the planned work. If you would like more detail on the full legislation around trees in Conservation Areas, you can find it on the Planning Portal and the GovUK website. 

! Warning It is an offence to fail to notify us. We may prosecute you, instruct you to replace any trees you remove, and you could also have to pay a large fine.

Apply for works to trees in a Conservation Area

If you want to check the applications and notifications for tree works in your area, you can find them on our Planning Register. Use the Advanced Search tool, and select Application Type; works to trees are at the bottom of the alphabetical list.

Tree survey reports

If you are proposing a development which will affect trees on your or adjoining properties, you will need to submit an arboricultural report (a tree survey) with your planning application. This will indicate if you plan on removing or reducing any trees, and how you will protect those you plan to retain, during construction works.

There is a statutory British Standard which states that the tree survey should be undertaken by a qualified arborist, and describes what it should include. If you are a tree care or development professional, you may want to order a copy of British Standard 5837: 2012 'Trees in relation to design, demolition and construction' from the British Standards Institute.

A summary of the details to include

An overview

  • Topographical survey showing the exact locations of trees
  • Reference number for each tree or group
  • List of species, by common name

Dimensions for each tree

  • Height
  • Stem diameter
  • Branch spread
  • Height above ground level of the first branch or canopy

The condition of each tree

  • The life stage, for example, young, semi-mature
  • General observations on the structural and physiological condition 
  • A statutory grading of the removal or retention category 
  • An estimate of remaining life, in years (<10, 10+, 20+, 40+)

Plans for each tree

  • Preliminary management recommendations
  • A Tree Constraints Plan
  • An Arboricultural Implication Assessment
  • A Tree Protection Plan
  • Arboricultural Method Statements where necessary
